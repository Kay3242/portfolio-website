The Theory of Space is a short stop motion film that reinterprets Georges Perec’s Species of Spaces through handcrafted typography and analog textures. Combining paper cutouts, frame-by-frame animation, and layered sound design, the film explores how language occupies, disrupts, and reshapes physical space. Its non-linear, VHS-inspired aesthetic reflects the fragmented nature of memory and the poetic potential of everyday environments.

This opening frame poses a deceptively simple question that underpins the entire stop motion film: What defines space? By presenting it on a whiteboard with analog textures and VHS overlays, the piece immediately blurs boundaries between physical, conceptual, and textual space—inviting viewers to question how meaning and form intersect in everyday environments.

I prototyped several animation styles using cut-out paper text. Techniques like “Stretch,” “Shift,” and “Top to Bottom” explored the motion, destruction, and tumbling of letters to exaggerate their spatial presence and rhythm across frames.

To enhance the retro feel, I combined stop motion with Adobe After Effects and Premiere Pro techniques—such as Rotobrush masking, camera tracking, and VHS-style overlays. These effects layered in a sense of nostalgia and imperfection that echoed the analog, textural essence of the project.

The soundscape blends diegetic and non-diegetic elements, including street noise, ambient audio, and a self-narrated monologue. These layers were carefully timed to mirror the film’s pacing and further question how sound and space intersect in visual storytelling.

Drawing from Perec’s experimental formatting and spatial philosophy, I investigated how text can occupy, disrupt, and reshape physical and visual space. The project reflects on how everyday environments are structured—physically, narratively, and emotionally.
